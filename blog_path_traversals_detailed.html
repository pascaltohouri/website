Relaxing Assumptions on the Way to More General Experiment Paths

Nov 27, 2025 •

This blog post follows the problem established in A Quick Formal Description of Optimal Path Traversals.

Restating the Problem

In the preceeding articles, every machine learning experiments was described by a path $\gamma$ through the semantic vector space $\mathbb{R}^n$. We formalized this as:

$$ \gamma: [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}^n, \quad \text{where } \gamma(a) = \text{Start}, \ \gamma(b) = \text{End} $$

This formalism provided a neat topological view of experimentation. However, it relied on a critical, often unrealistic assumption: that we know where we are going. In the clean mathematics of the calculus of variations, the endpoint $\gamma(b)$ is fixed boundary condition. In the messy reality of Machine Learning research, $\gamma(b)$ is the unknown optimal model we are trying to discover.

Relaxing the Fixed Endpoint Assumption

To make our theory useful, we must relax the assumption of a fixed destination. We are not solving a Boundary Value Problem; we are exploring an open space.

When we remove the fixed endpoint $\gamma(b)$, the nature of the traversal changes. The "optimal path" is no longer defined by the minimal action required to reach a specific coordinate. Instead, it is defined by the gradient of discovery. At any point $\gamma(t)$, the optimal next step is not necessarily the one that moves linearly toward a known solution, but the one that maximizes the rate of information gain.

This shifts our perspective from Interpolation (finding the best path between knowns) to Extrapolation (venturing into the void to find the unknown).

Dynamic Plotting and Local Utility

If the endpoint is unset, the path must be plotted dynamically. The "map" is effectively being drawn as we walk it. The utility function $f_t(\mathbf{x}_t)$ becomes a local heuristic—a flashlight beam illuminating only the immediate surroundings in the semantic manifold.

This dynamic plotting raises a significant question: Does the lack of a fixed endpoint require a completely separate mathematical framework?

Standard path optimization relies on global knowledge of the space. Dynamic traversal relies on local sensing and active decision-making. The topology of a space explored by an agent (Active Learning) is distinct from the topology of a space traversed by a projectile. It is likely that a separate article will be needed to formalize the specific geometry of these "exploratory" paths, where the metric tensor itself might be a function of the agent's uncertainty.

The Ultimate Goal: Generalist Active Learning

Why bother with this topological pedantry? Why fuss over the definitions of paths and manifolds?

We are not simply trying to train better classifiers for specific tasks. These essays are foundational steps toward a larger objective: building a Generalist, Multimodal, State-of-the-Art Active Learning Model.

Such a model acts as an autonomous traveler in experiment space. It cannot rely on human engineers to set the coordinates for every journey. It must understand the geometry of the semantic space itself to navigate efficiently—selecting its own data, tuning its own hyperparameters, and traversing from ignorance to generalization with minimal compute. To build this model, we must first understand the ground it walks on.
