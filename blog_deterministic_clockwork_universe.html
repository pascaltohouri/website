Paths Through the Clock-Work Universe

Dec 04, 2025 •

Purpose: Not all experiment paths through the space of information are equally desirable. This post adds a preference layer to an underspecified autoregressive model to drive experiment traversals.

Path Preference

In the last blog post, we described an autoregressive update for moving between experiments in the semantic vector space. This process lets us trace a path of experiments whose coordinates we could decode to recover a full machine learning setup. The autoregressive model was underspecified because it did not specify a preference over new experiments. Since we seek a specific type of experiment that maximises our utility (e.g. via high performance, efficiency, or interpretability), we can use information-theoretic structure to steer ourselves towards those optimal experiments in the SVS. In simple terms, if logical laws make a conclusion consequent to its premise, and physical laws restrict what is possible, these laws help us choose new experiments from old experiments.

The Clock-Work Universe

Let’s suppose the apparent complexity of the physical world is generated by a relatively small set of underlying mechanisms, like clockwork hidden behind the face of a watch. To understand an experiment path, we would inspect those gears which prescribe the path’s update rule. The autoregressive model in the prior blog post traces a deterministic path:

\[ X_{t+\Delta} = f(X_t). \]

Our typical interpretation is that the laws of nature (physical, informational, and computational) are the cogs that control the path. These laws restrict which moves are feasible and confine us to a subregion of the SVS.

In a fully determined universe, the feasible successor set collapses to a singleton and our experiment traversal is destined, at every moment, to move to the unique next compatible state. Preferences do not override the path from the outside; they are either (i) a deterministic part of the universe’s machinery, ostensibly directing which experiment we run, or (ii) a purely evaluative lens we apply to the space of counterfactual experiments. In either case, the clockwork universe does not permit free will (in the libertarian sense), preferences just assign higher or lower values to points (or subpaths) that are already mechanically reachable. So, humans are like clockwork automatons, bound to carry out experiments in a pre-determined sequence, and understanding the laws of nature completely would let us predict an experiment’s progression.

Physical Determinism

For a long time, humans thought this deterministic clockwork model accurately described the whole universe, not just the notionally plausible space of experiments. It was assumed that the laws of nature drove us mechanically from the past to the future. In fact, under standard interpretations of quantum mechanics, the future is not fully determined, so the clockwork model is an approximation, not a literal description of reality.

Still, we can use the approximate model to make some valuable claims. Notice that not all states of the universe are physically possible (e.g., pigs can’t fly), so there is a space of notional universes and a sub-region therein for the universes that are consistent with the laws of physics. Applying these laws whittles down the set of notional universes to a smaller feasible region. We can similarly apply logical or probabilistic laws to prohibit “illegal” moves. For example, suppose we want to reason about a computer calculation, which is infeasible due to its extensive resource demands. We can rule the calculation out as physically inconsistent, but still postulate that, if we had enough resources, the calculation would be logically consistent.

In discrete time, these laws create a transition map \(f\) from past to present. In continuous time, the same idea is expressed as a vector field \(v\) on the SVS, \(\frac{dX}{dt}=F(X)\), prescribing world state flows. It was Laplace who intimated that an intelligent entity that perfectly understood the universe’s cogs would know these vector field flows and could trace paths through them to determine past and future states of the current universe. Importantly, because a machine learning experiment is an informational sub-structure of a universe-state, the same cogs that constrain world evolution also constrain experiment-to-experiment moves. The experiment path is therefore a controlled traversal inside the physically admissible region carved out by natural laws.

Research Relevance

Literally stuffing the laws of nature and reason into a formal description of an experiment’s state may therefore constrain the experiment’s evolution. For example, in the SVS, encoding state traversal rules that approximate the laws of nature will tell us that experiment \(e_1\) cannot map to the physically infeasible \(\{e_2,\dots,e_N\}\). Layering laws to obtain a complete deterministic description would resolve the process of experiment selection. So, the ORBIT model should articulate and encode as many path-constraining rules to eliminate bad candidate experiments and simplify search.
